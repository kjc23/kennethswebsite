Micah 2

@February 8, 2024

Theme:

The people of Israel (specifically the leaders) do not fear God and have forgotten/twisted His law so God will bring destruction to them, but he will save those that are faithful to Him.

Aim:

  • Be careful who you listen to about God's judgment / don't listen to those who twist God's word about judgment
  • Have a greater appreciation of God's justice, his holiness, that he won't let it go unpunished, that God is a God who judges.
  • Remain faithful to the God who promises to save.

Opening Question

Why do we feel so deeply for those who are oppressed? Whether it be on a small scale (ex. school bullies) or a large scale.

Potential answers:

It is underserved. It is an unfair power dynamic. The oppressor wields their power in a way that is completely opposite to God’s desire/design.

Initial Charges and Sentencing (v1-5)

  1. Who is Micah speaking to in these verses?

    Evil doers of Israel (potentially those in power)

    Focus on v1-2

    1. What are these people doing? And why?

      (Have people point at specific verses)

      The people are planning evil simply because they can (v1)

      They covet and seize fields and houses. They defraud people of their homes and their inheritance (v2)

    1. Why would it be such an egregious thing to steal people’s fields and homes? Especially in the context of a society in 7th century BC.

      Ancient Israel was an agrarian society and people lived off of the money made from their fields. So effectively, their livelihoods and their homes are being stolen.

    1. When are these deeds carried out and why is it significant?

      v1: “At morning’s light”

      These evil deeds are carried out in the light of day. It is possible that people were ‘legally’ cheated out of their possessions and homes.

      Israel has descended so far that nobody bats an eye when people’s homes and lives are stolen in broad daylight.

Notice the THEREFORE in v3 (what is the therefore there for?)

  1. What do verses 3-5 reveal about God and His character?

    These verses show that God is a just God and His anger is kindled against those who make a mockery of His law and His creation.

    It also shows that God is faithful to His covenant. He has previously told the Israelites that if they fall away from Him and do not uphold their end of the covenant, they will suffer greatly.

  1. Is God’s punishment proportionate to the Israelites’s crimes?

    Hopefully people will say yes.

    Want to encourage thought about the seriousness of sin and God’s unflinching destruction of sin.

  1. What is meant in verse 5 by “you will have no one in the assembly of the Lord to divide the land by lot?” (Could cut and just explain for sake of time)

    Context: In Joshua, the tribes of Israel received different allotments of the promised land by the casting of lots.

    This verse means that the evildoers in Israel will have no allotment in the assembly of the Lord. They will not be in the presence of the Lord.

The False Prophets’s Retort (v6-7)

  1. What does the response of the false prophets reveal about them?

    They do not fear God.

    They do not understand God’s law, yet they believe they do.

    v7 is a callback to Deuteronomy 6:18 and the false prophets are quoting it claiming to be upright in the eyes of the Lord.

    The false prophets seem to have convinced themselves that they have done no wrong in the sight of God and are unwilling to listen to anything else.

Reiteration of Crimes and Punishment (v8-11)

  1. How are these verses similar to verses 1-5?

    These verses reiterate the evil that the people of Israel are doing and the way in which God will punish them for it.

    Focus on v10-11

    1. Why does Micah tell the oppressed to “Get up, go away!” What is the current state of Israel?

      Micah says that Israel is no longer a place for rest. It has become completely defiled and is beyond remedy.

    1. Why is the only prophet Israel would listen to one that prophesies plenty of beer and wine?

      It is not necessarily because Israel is a nation of drunkards, it is because this is a prophet that would not tell them things they don’t want to hear. The Israelites are only interested in being told what they want to hear.

      Micah is poking fun at the spiritual state of Israel. They have no care for anything outside themselves.

    1. What are we tempted to listen to that is not God?

      Discuss.

God’s Deliverance (v12-13)

  1. Who has God promised deliverance to?

    Those that are faithful to Him. The remnant.

  1. Why does Micah call them ‘the remnant?’

    God’s people are the remnant because they have to go THROUGH the judgement that is coming upon Israel. There is no way around God’s wrath but for those who are faithful, God promises that they will be saved and kept in the midst of His judgement of Israel.

  1. We have seen this analogy of the sheep and the shepherd throughout the Bible (but it bears repeating). What is the significance of it? Why are God’s people like sheep? And how is He our shepherd?

    *(Could possibly cut this question)

    We often get a warm feeling from the thought of being sheep and Jesus being our shepherd which is good. However the analogy is deeper than that as well. Sheep are dumb and tend to stray from the shepherd and the shepherd steers the sheep back into the right direction (often forcefully).

Application Questions

  1. How do we avoid being like the people of Israel and only listening to the things we want to hear?
  1. How does this vision of God that we've seen in ch2 have an impact on our own relationship with Him, our evangelism?
